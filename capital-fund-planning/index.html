Context

City Transit Authority Capital Improvement Program

The City Transit Authority (CTA) operates one of the region's largest public transit systems — serving over 1 million daily riders across rail, subway, light rail, and bus networks. In FY2025, the CTA's board approved a $922.8 million annual capital budget, part of an all-time-high $14 billion 12-year Capital Program enabled by the federal Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA).

In this exercise you play the role of a capital budget analyst at the City Transit Authority (CTA). The board has approved a 5-year, $1.575 billion Capital Improvement Plan (FY2025–FY2029). Your job is to build the financial architecture behind that plan: scheduling project expenditures, mapping out funding sources, modeling bond debt service, and assessing the impact on the authority's operating budget.

What is a Capital Improvement Plan?

A Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) is a multi-year schedule that identifies needed capital projects, estimates their costs, and aligns them with projected funding. Transit agencies use CIPs to:

  • Communicate investment priorities to elected boards and the public
  • Sequence projects to match funding availability (especially federal grant timelines)
  • Satisfy federal requirements for State of Good Repair tracking

Task 2

Funding Source Allocation

Pending

Capital projects are rarely funded from a single source. Transit agencies blend federal formula grants, competitive federal discretionary grants, state assistance, and locally-raised bond proceeds. For each fiscal year, you will calculate how much comes from each source — and determine the local share that must be bond-financed.

Funding Source Rules for This Exercise

  • Federal Formula Funds (FTA §5307/§5337): 40% of annual capital need. These are formula-based allocations received annually based on ridership and route miles.
  • PA State Capital Assistance (Act 89): 20% of annual capital need. Pennsylvania's dedicated transit capital funding stream.
  • Federal Discretionary Grants (IIJA): Lump-sum competitive grants — $80M awarded in FY2025 (MFL railcar contract), $45M in FY2027 (trolley signal system). These are given; do not calculate.
  • Local Bond Financing: The remainder after all other sources. This is what the authority must borrow.

Task 3

Bond Debt Service Schedule

Pending

The authority will issue $505,000,000 in revenue bonds to cover the 5-year local share identified in Task 2. These bonds carry a 4.50% annual interest rate and a 20-year term, with equal annual debt service payments (level annuity). Your task is to build the complete 20-year amortization schedule.

How Bond Amortization Works

Each year the authority makes a fixed annual payment that covers both interest and principal. In early years, most of the payment is interest (because the outstanding balance is high). Over time, as the balance falls, less interest accrues and more of each payment retires principal — even though the total payment stays the same.

  • Interest portion: Outstanding balance × annual rate
  • Principal portion: Fixed payment − interest
  • Ending balance: Beginning balance − principal repaid
Formula G — Annual Debt Service Payment (PMT)
Annual Payment = PMT(rate, term, −bond amount)
=ABS(PMT(C3,D3,−B3))  →  place result in cell E3
B3 = bond amount ($505,000,000); C3 = rate (0.045); D3 = term (20). PMT returns a negative value (outflow), so wrap with ABS(). Your answer: $38,822,453 per year.
Formula H — Interest Portion
Interest = Beginning Balance × Interest Rate
=B6*$C$3  —  absolute reference to rate cell; drag down all 20 rows
Formula I — Principal Portion
Principal = Annual Payment − Interest
=C6−D6  —  (Annual DS minus Interest); drag down all 20 rows
Formula J — Ending Balance
Ending Balance = Beginning Balance − Principal Repaid
=B6−E6  —  Year 1 only; subsequent years: beginning balance = prior ending balance
Year 20 ending balance should equal $0 (within a few cents of rounding). Use this as your built-in check.

Pending

Bond debt service is not a capital expense — it is an operating expense paid from the authority's annual operating budget. Understanding how debt service grows relative to the operating budget is critical for long-range fiscal health. In this task you model the debt service burden over the first five years of the CIP.

Key Parameters for Task 4

  • FY2025 Operating Budget: $1,740,000,000 (CTA FY2025 approved budget)
  • Annual Budget Growth Rate: 3.0% per year (driven by labor contracts, fuel, and maintenance inflation)
  • Annual Debt Service: $38,822,453 (from Task 3 — fixed for the full 20-year term)
Formula K — Operating Budget (Year-Over-Year Growth)

Discussion

Analysis & Policy Questions

Use your completed spreadsheet and the data from the four tasks to address the following questions. These are designed for written response or in-class discussion.

In FY2025, the federal leverage ratio is 8.55x — meaning the authority attracts $8.55 in federal dollars for every $1 of local bond financing. In FY2026 and later years without discretionary grants, the ratio falls to approximately 1.0x.

Questions to consider:

  • What accounts for this dramatic variation in leverage across years?
  • If a large discretionary grant is awarded in Year 1 but the project takes three years to build, how does grant timing affect cash flow and fund balance management?
  • What risks does an authority face if it builds a capital plan that depends heavily on competitive federal grants that may or may not be awarded?
  • How should a capital plan treat uncertain discretionary grants — as committed funding or as a contingency?

The authority issued 20-year bonds to finance a mix of assets with very different lifespans:

  • Railcars: 30–40 year useful life
  • Track & Infrastructure: 30–50 years
  • Bus Fleet: 12–15 years
  • Technology & Systems: 5–10 years

Questions to consider:

  • For which projects does a 20-year bond term make sense? For which is it too long or too short?
  • What problems arise when you finance a 10-year asset with a 20-year bond? What about financing a 40-year asset with a 10-year bond?
  • How might rating agencies or bond covenants constrain the term of financing for short-lived assets?

The debt service-to-operating-budget ratio ranges from 2.23% in FY2025 to 1.98% in FY2029. Transit bond rating agencies typically flag affordability concerns when this ratio exceeds 10–15%.

Questions to consider:

  • On its face, the CTA's position appears comfortable. What assumptions in your model are most critical to this conclusion?
  • The CTA already carries significant legacy debt from prior capital programs. How would you incorporate existing debt service obligations into this analysis?
  • If the operating budget growth rate slowed from 3% to 1% (e.g., due to a recession), how would the DS ratio change by FY2029? Recalculate and compare.
  • What non-financial factors (political pressure, labor contracts, federal compliance requirements) might constrain the authority's ability to manage its debt burden?

Pennsylvania's Act 89 capital assistance is funded partly by liquid fuels tax revenues, which have been declining as electric vehicles displace gasoline-powered cars.

Scenario: Suppose Act 89 capital assistance is reduced from 20% to 15% of annual capital needs for FY2027–FY2029.

  • How much additional local bond financing would be required for each affected year?
  • What would the new total bond amount be? Recalculate the annual debt service using the PMT function.
  • How much additional interest would the authority pay over the full 20-year life of the expanded bond issue?
  • At what point does a state funding cut become a structural threat to capital program delivery?

Major capital projects — new railcars on the Market-Frankford Line, trolley modernization — primarily benefit riders on specific corridors. Yet bond debt service is repaid from system-wide revenues: fares paid by all riders, state subsidies, and local government contributions.

Questions to consider:

  • Is it equitable for riders on unaffected routes to help pay for improvements they will not directly use?
  • How does the geographic and demographic distribution of the CTA's ridership affect the equity implications of this capital plan?
  • What alternatives exist — dedicated revenue bonds, TIF districts, special assessment districts — that might better align who pays with who benefits?
  • How should public transit agencies balance system-wide equity goals with the need to invest heavily in the most-used (and often most-underserved) corridors?
