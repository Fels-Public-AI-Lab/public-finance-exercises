When the Money Runs Out: Cash Flow & Liquidity at a Housing Nonprofit

Organization: Harborview Community Development Corporation (fictional)  ·  Baltimore, MD
Fiscal Year: October 1 – September 30  ·  Data Period: FY2023–FY2025
Background

The Liquidity Trap

Harborview Community Development Corporation (HCDC) is a fictional Baltimore nonprofit providing affordable housing counseling, tenant advocacy, and small-scale home repair assistance in East Baltimore neighborhoods. Like most CDCs, HCDC does important work — and is almost always one bad month away from a cash crisis.

HCDC receives funding from five sources: a HUD Community Development Block GrantFederal pass-through grants distributed by Baltimore City; paid quarterly, a Maryland DHCD operating grant, two private foundation grants (paid annually), individual donations concentrated in December, and program fee revenue from workshops. These revenue streams arrive at different times, often with large gaps between them.

Meanwhile, expenses — salaries, rent, utilities, insurance — flow out steadily every month. This mismatch between lumpy revenue and steady expenses creates the core challenge you'll analyze in this exercise.

⚠ The Mission–Money Tension

A nonprofit can be financially healthy on paper (surplus at year-end, adequate reserves) while still experiencing months where it cannot make payroll. This exercise shows you why annual financial statements tell only part of the story — and why cash flow management is a board-level governance responsibility.

Monthly Net Cash Flow

The most fundamental question in cash management: did more money come in than go out this month? Net cash flowRevenue received minus expenses paid in a given period. Positive = surplus; negative = deficit. answers that question for each period.

Formula
Net Cash Flow = Revenue Received − Operating Expenses
Excel: =B4-C4
Column D. A negative result (shown in red by convention) means HCDC spent more than it received that month.

Task 02 Pending

Cumulative Cash Position

Knowing whether a single month was positive or negative is useful — but what really matters for organizational survival is the cumulative cash positionThe running total of cash available, starting from the opening balance. If this hits zero, the organization cannot pay bills.: how much cash does HCDC actually have on hand as each month ends?

Opening Balance + Running Sum

The first month's ending balance is the opening balance plus that month's net cash flow. Every subsequent month's balance is the prior month's ending balance plus the current month's net cash flow.

  • E4 (first month): Opening Balance + Net Cash Flow = $48,500 + D4
  • E5 onward: Prior month ending balance + current net cash flow = E4 + D5
Two-Part Formula
E4 = 48500 + D4
E5 = E4 + D5  (copy down through E27)
Excel E4: =48500+D4  |  Excel E5: =E4+D5
Do not copy E4 down — it has the opening balance hard-coded. Start copying from E5.

What to Do in Excel

1In cell E4, type: =48500+D4
2In cell E5, type: =E4+D5
3Copy E5 down through E27. E4 stays alone — do not overwrite it.
4Label column E as "CASH POSITION". If any cell is negative, it signals a cash crisis that month.
Need Help?
Conceptual hint: Think of a checking account. You start with a balance. Every month, deposits add to it and withdrawals reduce it. The cumulative cash position is exactly that bank balance at the end of each month. If it goes negative, HCDC would bounce checks — even if the annual budget looks balanced.
Formula detail: E4 is special because it includes the opening balance ($48,500). Type that directly. E5 just adds the current month's net cash flow to the prior month's ending balance. Because E5 references E4 (not the fixed $48,500), copying it down works correctly — each row adds its net cash flow to the row above.

E4: =48500+D4
E5: =E4+D5 (copy through E27)

Months of Liquidity

The cash position tells you the dollar balance. But finance officers think in terms of time: how many months could HCDC continue operating if revenue stopped entirely? This is the months of liquidityCash on hand divided by average monthly expenses. Industry standard: nonprofits should maintain 3+ months; 6+ months is considered healthy. ratio — the gold standard of nonprofit financial health assessment.

Formula
Months of Liquidity = Cash Position ÷ Average Monthly Expenses
Excel F4: =E4/AVERAGE($C$4:$C$27)
The AVERAGE uses absolute references ($C$4:$C$27) so the denominator stays fixed as you copy the formula down. Format column F as a number with 1 decimal place.

Worked Example — October 2023 (Row 4)

$0 ÷ $0/mo = 0.0 months

The nonprofit sector benchmark: 3 months = minimum safe threshold. 6 months = healthy. Watch how this ratio moves across HCDC's two fiscal years.

What to Do in Excel

1In cell F4, type: =E4/AVERAGE($C$4:$C$27)
2The dollar signs lock the average expense range. This is critical — without them, copying shifts the range and gives wrong answers.
3Copy F4 down through F27.
4Label column F as "MONTHS OF LIQUIDITY". Add conditional formatting: red fill for values below 1.0, yellow for 1.0–2.9, green for 3.0+.
Need Help?
Conceptual hint: Months of liquidity converts a dollar balance into a time horizon — which is far more meaningful to a board member. Saying "we have $38,000 in the bank" is abstract. Saying "we have 2.1 months of runway" is actionable. The average monthly expense figure smooths out seasonal variation and gives a stable denominator.
Absolute references: The $ signs in $C$4:$C$27 prevent the range from shifting when you copy the formula down. Without them, F5 would calculate =E5/AVERAGE(C5:C28) — wrong. With them, every row divides by the same average expense figure.
Exact formula for F4: =E4/AVERAGE($C$4:$C$27)

F4 should show approximately months. Copy through F27. Look for months where this drops below 1.0 — those are acute danger zones for the organization.

Analysis

Discussion Questions

Click each question to reveal a model response framework. These questions are designed for in-class discussion or written assignment.

1 Looking at your baseline model, identify the two months where HCDC's cash position was most precarious. What was happening with revenue in those months, and what does this reveal about the risk structure of grant-dependent nonprofits? ▼
Model Response Framework

Strong answers will identify the specific months (likely late summer in each fiscal year — August or September) when the cumulative drain from steady expenses has depleted reserves before the next major grant cycle arrives. Look for the pattern: large infusions in Oct/Nov, a March or April mid-year grant, and then a long dry stretch.

The Structural Insight

Grant-dependent nonprofits face a structural mismatch that is entirely predictable: their revenue arrives in discrete events (grant awards, foundation disbursements, year-end campaigns) while their expenses are continuous. The risk isn't that the organization will be insolvent over the year — it's that it will be illiquid in specific windows, even if the annual budget is balanced.

2 In Scenario A, the grant delay causes HCDC's months-of-liquidity ratio to drop significantly. As a board member, what two actions would you recommend to prevent this outcome in the future? ▼
Model Response Framework

Answers should engage with real nonprofit financial tools, not just generic advice. Two strong directions:

  • Operating reserve policy: The board should adopt a formal policy requiring 3–6 months of operating expenses in an unrestricted reserve fund, replenished annually. This creates a buffer that can absorb exactly this kind of delay.
  • Line of credit: Many nonprofits establish a bank line of credit specifically for cash flow bridging — not for deficit spending, but to cover the gap between when a grant is awarded and when it is disbursed. The line is drawn down in lean months and repaid when funds arrive.
Governance Connection

This is a board-level decision, not a staff decision. The board's finance committee should be reviewing a cash flow forecast (like the one you built) at least quarterly — not just the income statement and balance sheet.

3 HCDC's executive director argues the organization is "financially healthy" because it ended both fiscal years with a surplus. Is this claim accurate? What would you add to give the board a complete picture? ▼
The "Technically True, Practically Misleading" Problem

The ED is not wrong — year-end surpluses are real and meaningful. But they are also a lagging indicator. A nonprofit can post a surplus in September and have been unable to pay suppliers in July. The annual financial statements smooth over intra-year volatility.

What the Board Needs
  • Monthly cash flow actuals vs. projections (the model you built)
  • Months-of-liquidity trend over the year, not just at year-end
  • A 12-month forward-looking cash flow projection updated quarterly
  • Clear board-adopted thresholds: what triggers an emergency finance committee meeting?

Financial health is a function of both solvency (do assets exceed liabilities?) and liquidity (can we pay our bills right now?). Both matter.
